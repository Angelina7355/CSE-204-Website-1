Women in Game Development: Unequal on and Behind the Screen

Author: Angelina Viviano

Date Published: 09/24/2025

Work Environment

In a world where nearly half of video game consumers are women, one might expect a similar ratio within the industry that creates those games. Unfortunately, that is not the case. Women make up only about 25% of game developers, and retention rates for women are significantly lower than for men. A toxic workplace culture, lack of female role models, and unequal pay all contribute to this problem. Women are frequently held to higher standards just to be seen as equal to their male colleagues, while still earning only 94 cents for every dollar men make in similar roles.

Female Protagonist Representation

For decades, mainstream games have been designed primarily for male audiences. Male protagonists are the majority, while female characters are often reduced to secondary roles, stereotypes, or sexualized. However, this imbalance is improving slowly: In 2020, 18% of games featured female protagonists compared to 23% with male leads. But this quantity does not guarantee authenticity. Because most women in games are still written by men, their stories can feel inauthentic or fall into harmful tropes, reducing the quality of representation.

Even Fictional Women Face Inequalities

There is a long-standing misconception that games with female leads won't sell. For example, when The Last of Us was released, Sony initially resisted putting Ellie (the female protagonist) on the cover, fearing it would hurt sales. Naughty Dog refused, and the game ended up being a worldwide success.

Female characters also face unique issues: oversexualization and disproportionate violence. Lara Croft in Tomb Raider, for example, has been put through threats of sexual assault and extreme violence that likely wouldn't have been written if she were a man. These patterns reinforce the unequal treatment of women— even in fictional worlds.

Relevance to Game Creators

  • Audience Disconnect: With women making up nearly half of players, failing to represent them authentically is a missed creative and financial opportunity.
  • Workplace Influence: When women are not included in writing rooms or design teams, their voices are absent from decisions about representation. This often results in developers recycling stereotypes, damaging their credibility and alienating part of their audience.
  • Backlash & Harrassment: When developers do push for women and/or minority protagonists, they often face online outrage, review bombing, and sometimes even personal harassment. This backlash can discourage studios and independent developers from taking risks on creating inclusive characters.
  • Industry Growth: Studies show that diverse teams produce more creative and profitable products. Supporting women in game development not only promotes equality but also makes for stronger, more innovative games.

Future Impact

Despite a long history of inequality, progress is being made. The proportion of female developers is gradually increasing, and more games are featuring female protagonists in meaningful roles. If studios continue to support authentic and respectful depictions of women — and hire women to create them — this could result in a positive cycle: more women entering the industry, which leads to better representation in games, which draws even more women to both play and create.
